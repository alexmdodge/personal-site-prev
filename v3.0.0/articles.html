New Perspectives on African-Centred Education in Canada

June 17, 2015

(January 2013 by George J. Sefa Dei and Arlo Kempf)

A reading from this book was assigned to us in class recently, and I thought the content was quite interesting. Africentric schooling is based around the idea that current school structures are dis-servicing students of all nationalities because of the engendered racism inherent in our society. The section focuses a great deal on the misconceptions of those who understand very little about the idea of Africentric schooling, and finalizes with a list of questions and answers about the system.

Africentric schooling is a school in which anyone who is interested may attend, but none are forced to. The schools teach the same curriculum but from an Africentric perspective, which strives to break free of the Eurocentric dogma typically praised in the public school systems. Another problem is that these views are typically perceived as the correct 'universal knowledge'. More often than not, public schools portray Africa as dangerous, disparaged, and uneducated. The author concludes that it makes sense that children of African lineage would not want to be associated with the image.

I gleaned a number of points from this article. The first is that we openly discuss how accepting we are of other cultures and their histories, but in reality there is still a deep vein of racism underlying all of our societal structures. Building on this, we really have no logical or moral reason to uphold these ideals, specifically in terms of religion. While most of the public school holidays are Christian, a massive majority are protestant, and other religions which are given no consideration at all. Another key point is that as much as we try to make these things accepted and integrated in our schools, we are failing, and need a new system from which to tackle the problems of cultural repression. A big piece is that we 'understand Indigenous knowledge as the legitimate epistemologies, ontologies, and axiologies of Indigenous and local peoples'. In short, we cannot degrade the beliefs and traditions of other cultures, just because they do not align with 'our' own.

hyperloop track to be built

June 15, 2015

(June 13, 2015 by Caroline Reid)

I have followed Elon Musk's work for some time, and the Hyperloop is just another project which both excites and baffles me. The basic idea of the Hyperloop is that it is an enclosed track which is kept in a partial vacuum state. Air will be used to keep the pod suspended above the track, and also propel the pod along with the help of some linear induction motors. The incredible thing is that the pod will potentially reach speeds of 1200 km/h. This is as fast as a jet. The goal is to create an accessible, efficient, and timely mode of transportation to bring people long distances. Especially for continental land travel, this would be ground breaking if scaled to larger proportions.

This article discusses that a test track will be built, which is 8 km long. This will be completed by 2017, and open to passengers by 2018. When Musk first proposed this idea, many thought it was another one of his extravagant ideas which would not be feasible with the technology we had. There are many obstacles to overcome, but this is certainly something we could see in the future. This just goes to show what happens when a scientist (one with a PhD in applied physics) becomes the head of a number of major production organizations with a lot of power.

new 3d printer technique

June 12, 2015

(March 17, 2015 by Dan Moren)

3D printing is an incredibly powerful technology, and is progressing at a breakneck speed. Whether you're a designer looking for a quick prototype, an engineer with a need for a unique component, or an astronaut on the international space station, 3D printing is here to stay. There are a number of different 3D printing methods. You can use lasers to melt a powdered metal into specific shapes, progressively heat a starch based powder, or squeeze out a layer of thin plastic one at a time. The problem with many of these methods is that they take a very long time. I have used a starch based layer printer, and it can take upwards of three to four hours to print something on 8 cm high. This article describes a new printing technique involving carbon liquid.

From the video you can see how much faster, and in some cases detailed, the process is compared to other types of 3D printing. The trick is the resin reacts strongly to UV radiation. So initially a surface is lowered into the resin, and as it is lifted out, the resin is exposed to a UV light source. By using a cross section of each layer, the UV source is adjusted to precisely cure the shape. It is known as the CLIP process, and produces 3D printed object in a fraction of the time, as well as producing prints which are smooth.

3D printing is an amazing technology and one which I think will revolutionize the biological field. There has been a good amount of research and work done in biological 3D. The most recent relating to this has dealt with the printing of skin grafts for burn patients . As this and the research on stem cells continues, I would not be surprised if in the future we are printing replacements organs for those in need of transplants.
