The Passion

There are two things that I love doing, and they are teaching and coding. I hope that one day I have a job in which I can do both. I would say that I love web development the most, but to be honest, it is all incredible fun. I have taken many of my experiences from teaching and applied it to a program, as well as an important technique or strategy from the programming education. Really it is all related, learning and doing are interconnected across every skill and discipline. I take this quite seriously in whatever capacity I'm working, as it makes you a better person to consider every possible perspective.

The Start

I attended Acadia University for my undergraduate degree, and finished with a double major in physics and computer science. Typically the field path for either of these degrees is to take an honours option, and then continue to a masters degree. When pondering my decision I panicked because I didn't know what to choose. How could I pick one field? I just loved it all too much. That's when it started to dawn on me that there was so much to learn, and that I never wanted to stop. I wanted to share, I wanted to explore, I wanted to develop , and I wanted to teach.

Philosophy

Everyone needs a philosophy. There are philosophies for everything, but in order to be engaged and successful in the path you've chosen you need a philosophy. Some time ago, when deciding what I wanted to do, I worried that I was diversifying too much, and that within my discipline I wasn't focusing enough on one topic. I came across the quote below this section. This has become my mantra, and something which I apply to both my teaching and programming practices.

In programming a new framework, standard, or tool comes out every week. You need to be constantly learning, adapting, and applying these new developments or you get left behind. This is what I love about this field, it is a constant learning process, and if the process feels stagnant, then something is wrong.

In my teaching practice I live by this, and encourage my students to do the same. Plato and Socrates believed in learning for the sake of learning to enrich your life. While some of their other practices may seem archaic, I think this primary ideology is fundamental in education. You always get the question, "Why are we learning this?". My response to this question is to ask students what they'll be doing four months next Tuesday at 3:10 PM? Obviously they don't know, nobody really knows. We learn because it enriches our lives and prepares us for the unknown. It allows us to be adaptable, to think critically, and to be well informed about the world we live in.
